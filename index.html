1. What marketing really is

In one sentence

Marketing is everything a company does to understand a customer first and then design the product, price, distribution and communication around that customer. Selling is pushing a product that already exists. Marketing is the opposite: pulling a product into existence because customers told you what they need.

In plain language

Imagine you want to open a bakery. Two different mindsets:

  • The selling mindset — bake whatever bread you like, then chase people down the street shouting about how fresh it is. If sales are slow, shout louder or drop the price.
  • The marketing mindset — walk around the neighbourhood first. Ask: who lives here? Are they diabetic? Do they want low-carb? Do they want french bread or baladi? What do they pay now? When do they buy? Then design the bakery around what you learned.

This is called outside-in thinking (start from the market) versus inside-out thinking (start from the factory). Dr. 3adel hammers the point: marketing starts before production, selling starts after production.

Needs vs wants vs demand. A need is a basic requirement — food, shelter, transport, status. A want is a specific desire shaped by culture and personality — shawarma vs. pizza, Toyota vs. BMW. Demand is a want backed by the ability to pay. Marketing doesn't invent needs; it reshapes wants and converts them into demand.

2. The five marketing management orientations

In one sentence

Companies have historically taken one of five stances toward the market. These stances evolved as markets matured: Production → Product (quality) → Selling → Marketing → Societal. The first three are inside-out (factory-first); the last two are outside-in (customer-first).

In plain language

OrientationCore beliefReal-world icon
ProductionMake it cheap and available. If you can produce it, it will sell. Used when demand outstrips supply.Henry Ford's Model T, Egyptian Nasr-era factories: Ideal, Atlas, Harbeya refrigerators, Fajr stoves.
Product (Quality)Make it better. Consumers will pay more for higher quality, performance, features. Constant improvement.Post-war Japan — Toyota, Sony, Honda. Rebuilt through obsession with quality.
SellingThe market is saturated; the customer won't buy unless we shout. Invest in promotion, discounts, payment facilities.Insurance salespeople, encyclopedia door-knockers, those real-estate cold-calls you get daily.
MarketingStart with the customer's needs; design everything around them. Build a long-term relationship.Apple, Starbucks, IKEA — companies obsessed with understanding the user's life.
SocietalBalance customer wants with society's long-term welfare: environment, health, vulnerable groups.Patagonia (environment), Dove (body image), Tesla (sustainability).

Why do you need all five? Because different markets and different eras demand different stances. Egypt in 1960 had a production-concept market (demand > supply). Egypt in 2026 has a marketing-concept market (consumer is overwhelmed by choice). A single company can evolve through stages as its industry matures.

Important slide bullet Dr. 3adel emphasised: the first three orientations (Production, Product, Selling) do NOT support long-term customer relationships — they end up creating dissatisfaction and switching. Only Marketing and Societal build loyalty.

3. Relationship marketing and one-to-one marketing

In one sentence

Relationship marketing means treating every customer interaction as one point in a long-term relationship, not a standalone transaction. One-to-one marketing is the extreme version: technology lets you tailor the product and the message to a single individual.

In plain language

The old Kodak slogan, "Press the button, we do the rest" — that's transactional. The customer gives money, gets a photo, goes home. Contrast it with Netflix: every show you watch teaches the system what to recommend next. Each interaction deepens the relationship and makes leaving the platform more painful. That is relationship marketing.

One-to-one marketing is when your product line has effectively one unit per customer. A bride gets her wedding car painted with her name; a private-banking client has an investment portfolio no one else in the world owns; a custom music playlist is generated for you alone. It's only possible because technology made it cheap to customise at scale (mass customisation).

4. Strategic marketing management — the 5 processes

Why this discipline exists — 8 environmental forces

Slide 2 of the Basic Concepts deck opens with the question "Why study Strategic Marketing Management?" and answers it with eight forces that make today's market a moving target. You can't just run yesterday's marketing plan and hope it still works [Slide: Basic Concepts s.2]:

  1. Changes in market structure — new entrants, consolidations, deregulation, e-commerce reshaping how a market is organised.
  2. Competition — globalisation means the threat is no longer just the shop next door; it's a Chinese or Turkish substitute landed at half your price.
  3. Cultural dynamics — values, family roles, religious adherence, gender norms shift over time. What sold in 2000 doesn't always sell now.
  4. Micro & Macro factors — micro: customers, suppliers, intermediaries, competitors. Macro: economy, demographics, technology, politics, law.
  5. Impact of technology — smartphones, AI, e-payments, social media advertising. Technology destroys old channels (Kodak film) and creates new ones (Netflix, ride-hailing).
  6. Marginal Propensity to Save / Consume — when income rises, do people save (luxury goods slow down) or consume (luxury goods boom)? Marginal propensity to consume applies most to basic goods; marginal propensity to save applies more to luxury goods.
  7. Changes in buyers' behaviour — younger generations buy online, read reviews, switch brands faster, ignore TV ads.
  8. Heterogeneity (تنوع المنتجات) — markets fragment into more sub-segments every year. The same product no longer satisfies "the average customer" — there is no average customer any more.

These eight forces are why the role of the marketer can't be static — they justify the existence of strategic marketing as a discipline distinct from day-to-day marketing operations.

How the marketing manager's role evolved

The marketing manager used to be a tactical executor — they ran campaigns, printed brochures, organised trade shows. Today they're a strategy maker. The shift Dr. 3adel verbalised in class [Lec 2 @ 42:17–42:31]: "From being only an implementer ... to being a maker of market organization. هو بقى الميكر، هو اللي بيصنع الاستراتيجي للشركة" — "they are now the maker, the one who builds the strategy for the company." [Slide: Strategic Marketing Pilot s.5–6]

Expanded responsibilities of the modern marketing manager include:

  • Charting the direction of the organisation — not just selling existing products but deciding which markets and products the company should pursue 5–10 years out.
  • Contributing to decisions that create and sustain a competitive advantage — pricing, branding, positioning calls that affect long-term performance, not quarterly sales.
  • Making strategic decisions, not just operational ones — they sit at the executive table.

This evolution is exactly why this course exists. A marketing manager today is one of the company's strategists, not just one of its tactical players.

Sizing the market strategically — the four levels

Before you commit a marketing plan, you need an honest picture of how big the market actually is. Slide 3 of the Basic Concepts deck draws this as four nested levels [Slide: Basic Concepts s.3]:

  1. Market Potential — the absolute ceiling. How many units could conceivably be sold in this market under ideal conditions to every possible buyer? (e.g., every adult in Egypt is a potential mobile-phone buyer.)
  2. Industry Sales Forecast — what the entire industry is expected to actually sell next period (less than market potential, because not every potential buyer will buy).
  3. Company Sales Forecast — what your firm expects to sell next period (your slice of the industry forecast = market share × industry sales).
  4. Unrealized Potential — the gap between Market Potential and your Company Sales. This is the white space — the room to grow if you can convert non-buyers into buyers, or steal share from competitors.

The strategic mindset is to constantly ask: where is my unrealized potential, and which marketing actions can convert it? A complacent firm forecasts company sales and stops there. A strategic firm forecasts industry sales, sees the gap, and plans how to close it.

In one sentence

Strategic marketing management is the five-step cycle a company runs (and re-runs as the environment changes) to decide what business it is in, what opportunities to pursue, which products to send to which markets, how to fund the plan, and how to recover when things go wrong.

In plain language

  1. Define the business, mission, and goals. What are we here for? Who do we serve? Where do we draw the line?
  2. Identify and frame growth opportunities. What's happening in the environment? Which opportunities fit our strengths? (This is where SWOT lives.)
  3. Formulate product-market strategies. Ansoff's grid: penetration, market development, product development, diversification. Plus the Marketing Mix (4Ps).
  4. Budget the resources. Translate the plan into money — operating budget (income statement) and financial budget (cash flow).
  5. Develop reformulation and recovery strategies. When the environment turns — war, pandemic, currency shock — rework the plan.

5. Business definition, mission, and goals

In one sentence

Define your business from the market (what the customer's need is), not from the product (what you happen to produce). This one shift protects you from going obsolete when technology changes the product.

In plain language

Kodak defined itself as "we make film." When digital photography arrived, Kodak died. If Kodak had defined itself as "we capture memories," it would have embraced digital cameras instead of fighting them.

Dr. 3adel gave four famous market-perspective redefinitions:

CompanyProduct perspective (narrow)Market perspective (wide)
ToyotaWe make cars.We provide transportation solutions — land, sea, air; trucks, buses, vans, forklifts.
Microsoft (Bill Gates)We sell software."We are not producing products, we are solving our customers' problems." This is literally where the word "solutions" entered the software industry.
DisneyWe run amusement parks / make films.We provide entertainment.
IKEAWe sell furniture."Home dress" — every single thing that goes into making a home a home: furniture, candles, bedsheets, even chlorine.

Why this matters: if you call yourself "a sweets shop" (El-Abd, El-Orouba), you'll lose when a café opens next door that also sells nuts, sweets, coffee. But if you define yourself as an entertainment venue, the café is a natural extension of your own business, not a threat.

Business definition answers three questions: (1) Who are our customers? (2) What needs do we satisfy? (3) With what means / technology?

The mission statement then formalises the business definition into a short public statement — capturing purpose, customers, products, markets, philosophy, technology. It is revisable as the business evolves.

Why a mission statement matters — 4 specific benefits [Slide: Strategic Marketing Pilot s.12]:

  1. Crystallizes management's vision of the organisation's long-term direction and character. Forces the leadership to put a stake in the ground.
  2. Provides guidance in identifying, pursuing, and evaluating market and product opportunities. When a new opportunity arises, the mission tells you whether it fits.
  3. Inspires and challenges employees to do those things that are valued by the organisation and its customers. A good mission rallies the team; a generic mission ("we deliver value to stakeholders") inspires nobody.
  4. Provides direction for setting business goals or objectives. The mission tells you what to measure; the goals tell you whether you're hitting it.

Goals convert the mission into measurable targets in three categories: Production (capacity, quality), Financial (ROI, cash flow, profit), Marketing (market share, sales volume, satisfaction, customer value).

6. Spotting opportunities — SWOT, distinctive competency, success requirements

In one sentence

A real growth opportunity exists only where your internal strengths (distinctive competencies) match the external environment's needs (success requirements of the market). SWOT is the 2×2 framework you use to see that match (or mismatch) clearly.

In plain language

Environmental opportunities have three typical sources: (1) unmet or changing customer needs, (2) unsatisfied buyer groups, (3) new means or technology for delivering value.

Distinctive competency = what you do best — skills, technologies, resources — that other companies cannot easily copy.

Success requirements = the basic tasks any company must perform in this market to compete. If your distinctive competency doesn't cover the market's success requirements, the opportunity is not yours — walk away.

SWOT arranges the diagnostic as a 2×2:

FavourableUnfavourable
Internal (you)Strengths — brand, money, location, technologyWeaknesses — outdated facilities, weak talent
External (world)Opportunities — new segments, tech shifts, crises creating demandThreats — currency shocks, war, substitute products

Dr. 3adel was emphatic: SWOT is mandatory for any long-term strategic plan.

Three diagnostic questions to ask once SWOT is on the page [Slide: Strategic Marketing Pilot s.22]:

  1. Which internal strengths represent distinctive competencies? Not every "strength" is a real differentiator. The question is: do these strengths compare favourably with what the market or industry actually requires for success? If a strength is widely shared by competitors, it isn't distinctive.
  2. Which internal weaknesses disqualify the organisation from pursuing certain opportunities? Some weaknesses are deal-breakers — no money for capex, no licence, no distribution reach in the target geography. These weaknesses should rule whole opportunity classes out of the picture early, before you waste planning effort.
  3. Does a pattern emerge from the SWOT? The most useful insight is rarely a single S, W, O, or T item — it's the cross-pairing. A Strength matched to an Opportunity = an offensive move. A Weakness matched to a Threat = an existential risk. Look for the pattern, not the items in isolation.

7. Product-market strategies — the Ansoff Matrix

In one sentence

Ansoff's 2×2 grid (Existing vs New products, Existing vs New markets) gives four growth strategies: Market Penetration, Market Development, Product Development, and Diversification — in increasing order of risk.

In plain language

Existing productsNew products
Existing marketsMarket Penetration — sell more of what you already have to customers you already reach.Product Development — invent new things for the customers you already have.
New marketsMarket Development — take your existing product to a geography / segment you don't yet serve.Diversification — new product, new market. Highest risk.

Each box has typical tactics:

  • Penetration: push current buyers to use more; steal competitors' buyers; stimulate trial.
  • Market Development: modify the offering, change distribution, new ad campaign. Internationally this means the four modes — Licensing, Exporting, Joint Venture / Strategic Alliance, Direct Investment.
  • Product Development has three modes: Product Innovation (brand-new offering, e.g., Apple iPad), Product Augmentation (improve an existing offering), Product Line Extension (different sizes / flavours / forms).
  • Diversification: brand-new product AND brand-new market. Often done by acquisition.

8. The Marketing Mix — 4Ps with the customer at the centre

In one sentence

Once you've chosen a market strategy, the 4Ps — Product, Price, Place, Promotion — are the four levers you actually pull. They are always designed around the customer, who sits in the centre of the picture.

In plain language

  • Product — what you sell (features, design, quality, branding, packaging, service).
  • Price — what the customer pays (list price, discounts, credit terms, allowances, skimming vs penetration pricing).
  • Place — how you distribute (channels, coverage, inventory, transport, locations).
  • Promotion — how you communicate (advertising, personal selling, sales promotions, PR, direct marketing, digital).

Service businesses extended the 4Ps to 7Ps by adding People (trained staff), Process (the steps that deliver the service), and Physical evidence (tangible cues of quality — Apple store design, hotel lobby).

A more recent reframe is the 4Rs: Relevance, Recognition, Rewards, Relationships — shifting emphasis from transactional push to loyalty and long-term value.

9. Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning (STP) — the core of this course

In one sentence

STP is the four-step customer-driven marketing-strategy engine: Segment the market into groups, Target the ones worth pursuing, Differentiate your offering, and Position it in the customer's mind. Using multiple segmentation bases together

You don't have to pick just one base. Slide 13's exact wording: "Multiple segmentation is used to identify smaller, better-defined target groups." [Slide: Kotler Ch.7 s.13]

Plain language: the more bases you combine, the smaller and sharper the segment becomes. Geographic + Demographic + Behavioural = "30–45-year-old women living in upper-Cairo, single-mum life-cycle stage, regular users of organic baby food, willing to pay premium prices." That's a far more actionable segment than just "women" or just "upper-Cairo residents." Marketers call this multivariate segmentation or geodemographic segmentation when geography + demographics combine. The trade-off: each extra base shrinks the segment size, so you have to balance precision against substantiality (one of the 5 effective-segmentation requirements below).

When the customer is another company — a refinery buying inhibitors, a hospital buying lab kits, a fleet manager buying tyres — you still segment, but with a different set of variables. Segmenting international markets

When you slice across countries rather than within one country, the segmentation variables shift again. Slide 15 names three factors [Slide: Kotler Ch.7 s.15]:

  1. Economic factors — the strength and structure of each country's economy (developed vs. developing, GDP per capita, currency stability, household disposable income). Selling premium cars makes sense in countries with high per-capita income; selling sachet detergent fits low-income markets.
  2. Political & legal factors — political stability, regulatory environment, tax regimes, import/export rules, intellectual-property enforcement. A country with unstable politics or sudden tariff swings is a riskier segment, even if its economics look attractive.
  3. Cultural factors — language, religion, social norms, values. The cultural variable Dr. 3adel highlighted most was religion — what's halal/haram, what may be advertised, dress codes, dietary restrictions, gender-mix in public spaces. Beef burgers don't sell in India; pork doesn't sell in the Gulf; alcohol advertising is illegal in Saudi Arabia; gender-segregated stores are the norm in some markets and unthinkable in others.

Inter-market segmentation

This one is easy to confuse with international segmentation, but it is the opposite move. Slide 16's exact wording: "Inter-market segmentation divides consumers into groups with similar needs and buying behaviors even though they are located in different countries." [Slide: Kotler Ch.7 s.16]

Plain language: instead of treating "Egypt" and "Germany" as two separate markets and tailoring to each, you find the same kind of customer in both countries and treat them as one global segment. The classic example is gamers: a 20-year-old PlayStation gamer in Cairo wants the same console, the same games, the same headset, the same payment methods as a 20-year-old gamer in Berlin or Tokyo. Sony can sell the same product to all of them with the same global marketing because the segment travels across borders.

Other classic inter-market segments: luxury-watch buyers (a Rolex buyer in Dubai and a Rolex buyer in New York behave the same way), Apple iPhone power-users, Marlboro smokers, business-class air travellers. Lexus deliberately targets wealthy consumers in many countries — that's an inter-market play.

Quick distinction to memorise:

  • International segmentation = country-by-country. Each country = one segment, with its own economic / political / cultural profile. You tailor to each country.
  • Inter-market segmentation = customer-type across countries. One segment, many countries. You treat the cross-border tribe as one market.

Requirements for effective segmentation (memorise this list — 5 items):

  1. Measurable — you can size the segment
  2. Accessible — you can reach them via a channel
  3. Substantial — the segment is large enough to be profitable
  4. Differentiable — the segments respond differently to marketing
  5. Actionable — you can design an effective programme for them

What is a "target market"?

Once you've sliced the market into segments, you don't serve every segment — you pick the ones you'll actually go after. Slide 18's exact wording: "A target market consists of a set of buyers who share common needs or characteristics that the company decides to serve." [Slide: Kotler Ch.7 s.18]

Three things to notice in that definition:

  • "A set of buyers" — not the whole market, just a chosen group.
  • "Share common needs or characteristics" — the segment must be cohesive; what unites them must be more important than what divides them.
  • "The company decides to serve" — targeting is an active choice. You're saying yes to one segment and no to others, and the no's are just as important as the yes's.

Evaluating market segments — the three criteria

Before you pick which segment(s) to chase, you have to evaluate each one. Slide 19 lists three evaluation criteria — every segment must be judged against all three before you commit [Slide: Kotler Ch.7 s.19]:

  1. Segment size and growth — is the segment big enough today, and is it growing? A small but fast-growing segment can be more attractive than a large but stagnant one. You don't want to sink a marketing budget into a shrinking pond.
  2. Segment structural attractiveness — how brutal is competition inside the segment? Borrow Porter's logic: are there many strong rivals already? Are there cheap substitutes the customer could switch to? Is it easy for new entrants to come in and erode prices? A segment that looks big and growing can still be unattractive if a price war is dragging margins to zero.
  3. Company objectives and resources — does this segment fit your strategy and your capability? A segment can be large, growing, and structurally attractive, but if your firm doesn't have the technology, brand permission, distribution reach, or capital to serve it, you should walk away. Only pursue segments where your distinctive competency matches the segment's success requirements (the same logic from §6).

A segment is "go" only when the answer to all three criteria is favourable.

Choosing your targeting strategy — the five factors

Once you've evaluated the segments and picked a candidate, the next decision is which of the four targeting strategies to apply. Slide 27 names five factors that drive that choice [Slide: Kotler Ch.7 s.27 ; Lec 3-2 @ 35:06]:

  1. Company resources — if your firm is small or cash-constrained, concentrated (niche) is the realistic play. Large firms with deep pockets can afford differentiated (many product lines + many marketing campaigns) or undifferentiated (one giant national rollout). Dr. 3adel: "limited company resource" [Lec 3-2 @ 18:11].
  2. Product variability — how easy is it to vary the product? Commodity-like products (water, sugar, plain biscuits) are uniform → undifferentiated. Highly variable products (phones, cars, cosmetics, fashion) lend themselves to differentiated because each variant has a real reason to exist.
  3. Product life-cycle stage — at introduction, when the category is brand-new and the market still has to learn what it even is, an undifferentiated single offer is usually best (one message, build category awareness). As the market matures and customers' tastes diverge, the firm shifts to differentiated (a variant for each emerging sub-segment).
  4. Market variability — how diverse are the customers themselves? If every buyer wants roughly the same thing (low variability), undifferentiated works. If buyers have visibly different tastes / needs / occasions / budgets (high variability), only differentiated or concentrated will satisfy them. Dr. 3adel: "market variability يعني درجة الاختلافات في السوق" [Lec 3-2 @ 36:16].
  5. Competitor marketing strategies — you don't choose your strategy in a vacuum. If every major competitor in the segment is already running differentiated with multiple variants, you usually have to match or you'll look impoverished and lose share. If competitors are running undifferentiated, you can sometimes win by being the first to differentiate. [Lec 3-2 @ 37:34]

Socially responsible target marketing

The last sub-topic of targeting (slide 28) asks an ethical question: just because a firm can target a segment, does that mean it should? [Slide: Kotler Ch.7 s.28 ; Lec 3-2 @ 37:36 — "في socially responsible"]

Some segments are vulnerable — they need protection from aggressive or harmful targeting. The two examples on the slide:

  • Children with potentially harmful or controversial products — cigarettes targeted at minors, sugary cereals with cartoon mascots, junk food advertised during kids' programming. Dr. 3adel told the Nike-children-labour story in class as the cautionary tale [Lec 3-2 @ 38:08–38:30] — a brand celebrating its global success made a film that accidentally exposed children working in their supply chain, and the brand was hit hard for child-labour abuse.
  • Internet abuses — predatory online targeting (manipulative ads, addictive games, dark-pattern paid loops, exploiting psychological weaknesses of teenagers, mining personal data without consent).

Other vulnerable segments often raised in the same discussion: addicts (alcohol, gambling, junk food), the elderly (financial scams, miracle-cure products), people in financial distress (predatory lending). The principle: a marketer is socially responsible not when they refuse to target these groups, but when they protect them from products that would damage their long-term welfare. This is the Societal Marketing orientation from §2 in action — customer wants and society's long-term welfare.

How to build differentiation and positioning — the three-step process

Differentiation and positioning are two halves of the same job: differentiation is the real-world change you make to the offer; positioning is where that change lives in the customer's mind. Slides 41–43 lay out a clean three-step recipe to build both [Slide: Kotler Ch.7 s.41–43]:

  1. Identify a set of possible competitive advantages. List every single way your offer could be different and better — across product, services, channels, people, image. Cast a wide net first; you'll narrow later.
  2. Choose the right competitive advantages. From that long list, pick the one (or two) that actually matter to the customer, that you can deliver consistently, and that competitors cannot easily copy. A "feature" only becomes a competitive advantage when the customer perceives it as valuable.
  3. Select an overall positioning strategy. Decide the value proposition (more benefit at higher price, or greater benefit at the same/lower price), then write the positioning statement that broadcasts that strategy to the market.

Step 1 — Identify possible advantages: the five bases of differentiation

Slide 41 lists five places you can look for a possible advantage [Slide: Kotler Ch.7 s.41]:

  • Product — features, performance, style, durability (Sharp AC's air-purifying tech).
  • Services — installation, after-sales, warranty. Classic example Dr. 3adel gave: Apple's 2010 iPhone-migration-from-Nokia service — they provided the cables and a free in-store device that moved your Nokia contacts / photos / emails onto your new iPhone. Nobody else was doing that. [Lec 3-2 @ 50:30]
  • Channels — store experience, digital integration, distribution reach.
  • People — staff quality. The attentive waiter vs. the distracted waiter — same restaurant, very different experience [Lec 3-2 @ 51:41].
  • Image — brand perception, symbols, logos, heritage. Rolex sells image as much as it sells timekeeping.

Step 2 — Choose the right advantage: what makes one "right"?

Not every difference is worth promoting. A good competitive advantage is:

  • Important — the customer actually values it.
  • Distinctive — competitors don't already deliver it (or they deliver it less well).
  • Communicable — you can explain it in one sentence.
  • Affordable — the customer can pay for the value it adds.
  • Profitable — you can deliver it at a margin.
  • Sustainable — competitors can't easily copy it. Today's advantage can be copied (Skechers copied Crocs; Huawei copied Apple). That's why the literature talks about sustainable competitive advantage — an advantage structured so competitors cannot replicate it cheaply.

Step 3 — Select an overall positioning strategy

Once you've chosen the advantage, you have to package it as a value proposition. The two phrases Dr. 3adel emphasised verbatim (and the friend's class notes capture them word-for-word):

  • "Greater value at the same or lower price." — you give the customer more benefit without charging more.
  • "Providing more benefit that justifies a higher price." — you charge more, but the customer feels they're getting their money's worth.

Positioning is the mental place. Positioning itself is the place your brand occupies in the customer's mind relative to competitors. It is not about your product; it is about their perception. Dr. 3adel's phrasing: "ضع نفسك في مكانة ذهنية مميزة" — "put yourself in a distinctive mental place" [Lec 3-1 @ 31:44].

Slide 29 unpacks "the customer's mind" into three specific elements that together form a product's position [Slide: Kotler Ch.7 s.29]:

  • Perceptions — what the customer thinks about the brand on important attributes (quality, price, reliability). E.g., "BMW = engineered to drive."
  • Impressions — what the customer remembers from past exposure (ads, packaging, store visits, word of mouth). E.g., the impression of an Apple Store as bright, minimal, premium.
  • Feelings — the emotional response the brand triggers (pride, comfort, excitement, trust, fear). E.g., wearing a Rolex = pride; flying first class = status; using Amazon = convenience.

The takeaway: positioning is built from all three layers at once. A purely rational positioning (perceptions only — "we have the lowest price") is fragile; the strongest positioning combines all three (perceptions + impressions + feelings).

10. Budgeting and the marketing audit

Budgeting

Slide 33 of Deck 2 defines a budget verbatim as: "a formal, quantitative expression of an organisation's planning and strategy initiatives expressed in financial terms." A well-prepared budget meshes and balances the firm's Financial, Production, and Marketing resources so that overall organisational goals are attained [Slide: Strategic Marketing Pilot s.33]. Two components [Slide: Strategic Marketing Pilot s.34]:

  • Operating Budget (pro-forma income statement) — focuses on the income statement: revenues, costs, profit.
  • Financial Budget — focuses on the cash position: the effect the operating plan and other initiatives will have on the company's cash.

The Marketing Audit

A marketing audit is a comprehensive, systematic, independent, and periodic examination of the company's marketing environment, objectives, strategies, and activities — with the goal of recommending a plan to improve performance.

It answers two questions — memorise these exactly:

  1. Are we doing the right things? (Is our strategy aimed at the right markets, customers, value propositions?)
  2. Are we doing things right? (Are we executing the strategy efficiently — budgets, processes, channels?)

Both questions are necessary: a company can execute a flawed strategy brilliantly, or execute a brilliant strategy badly — either way the outcome suffers.

The Marketing Plan

The marketing plan is the document that pulls everything together. Slide 37's exact definition: "A formal written document that describes the context and scope of an organisation's marketing effort to achieve defined goals or objectives within a specified future time period." [Slide: Strategic Marketing Pilot s.37]

Two characteristics of the plan:

  • Focus — the plan can be written for a whole business, a single product, or a specific brand. Each level has its own document.
  • Time dimension — short-run plans typically cover one year (the annual marketing plan), long-run plans span multiple years (3–5 years is typical for strategic plans).

How the plan relates to everything else in this course: the plan is the output of all five processes from §4. Process 1 (mission/goals) goes in the front. Process 2 (SWOT/opportunities) shapes the strategy section. Process 3 (Ansoff + 4Ps) produces the action plan. Process 4 (budget) gives it numbers. Process 5 (recovery) is the contingency section. The marketing audit then loops back to evaluate execution against the plan.

Why this matters for your exam

  • Expect a short-answer: "What are the two questions the Marketing Audit helps answer?"
  • Expect a T/F distinguishing the Operating Budget (income statement) from the Financial Budget (cash position).
  • Expect a definition-recall on the Marketing Plan ("formal written document … defined goals … specified future time period") or on its two characteristics (focus + time dimension).

  • Marketing starts at the market. The company studies customer needs — tastes, colours, prices, designs — BEFORE producing. It is an "outside-in" view: "I produce what the customer wants." Example: Starbucks designing every new drink and every store experience around observed customer preferences.
  • Key consequences: Selling focuses on volume this quarter; Marketing builds long-term relationships and loyalty. Selling treats the customer as a target; Marketing treats the customer as the centre of the 4Ps.

2. Explain the four Marketing Management Orientations with one real-world example of each.
Model answer:
  • Production — consumers favour products that are available and affordable. Focus is high-volume, low-cost production. Example: Henry Ford's Model T; the Egyptian Ideal / Atlas / Harbeya factories of the Nasr era. Used when demand exceeds supply.
  • Product (Quality) — consumers favour products with the highest quality, performance, and features. Example: Post-war Japan (Toyota, Sony, Honda) rebuilt through quality obsession.
  • Selling — consumers will not buy unless the company mounts a large-scale selling and promotion effort. Example: insurance cold-callers; door-to-door encyclopaedia sales; aggressive real-estate calls.
  • Marketing — the organisation achieves its goals by knowing the target market's needs and delivering the desired satisfaction better than competitors. Example: Apple, Starbucks — companies designed around customer experience.
  • (Bonus) Societal Marketing — decisions balance customer wants + company needs + consumer long-term welfare + society's long-term welfare. Example: Patagonia (environment), Tesla (sustainability).

4. Define SWOT analysis and explain why Dr. 3adel called it "mandatory" for any long-term strategic plan.
Model answer: SWOT is a 2×2 framework that forces an organisation to simultaneously evaluate its internal capabilities (Strengths and Weaknesses) and its external environment (Opportunities and Threats). An organisational growth opportunity exists only where a good internal-external fit can be engineered — internal strengths matching external opportunities, and internal weaknesses not disqualifying the firm from pursuing those opportunities. It is mandatory for long-term plans because without it, strategy degenerates into either wishful thinking (chasing opportunities without the capability) or capability-dumping (deploying strengths where no opportunity exists). Dr. 3adel stressed this at Lec 2 @ 1:45:59. Typical strengths: brand, cash, location, technology. Typical opportunities: COVID mask demand, currency gaps, new consumer segments. 5. Why define a business from the market perspective rather than the product perspective? Illustrate with at least two of Dr. 3adel's canonical examples.
Model answer: A product-perspective definition ("we make X") locks the firm to a single technology. When that technology changes, the firm dies with it (Kodak defined itself as "we make film" and could not survive digital photography). A market-perspective definition ("we satisfy Y customer need") is flexible — the firm can change the means while keeping the customer base intact. Canonical examples Dr. 3adel used:
  • Toyota: not "we make cars" but "we provide transportation solutions" — trucks, buses, vans, forklifts, land / sea / air.
  • Microsoft: Bill Gates's own framing — "we are not producing products, we are solving our customers' problems." This is where the word "solutions" entered the software industry.
  • Disney: not "we make films" but "we provide entertainment."
  • IKEA: not "we sell furniture" but "home dress" — every single thing that goes into a home.
The business definition should answer three questions: (1) who are our customers? (2) what needs do we satisfy? (3) by what means / technology? [Slide: Kerin Ch.1 s.9-10; Lec 1 @ 46:29–55:15]

7. Compare Differentiation and Positioning. How are they linked?
Model answer: Differentiation and Positioning are two linked but distinct steps in the STP engine:
  • Differentiation is the real change you make to your market offering — something measurable on one of five bases: product, services, channels, people, image. Example: Sharp designed AC units with plasma-cluster air purification (product differentiation); Apple launched the free in-store Nokia-to-iPhone migration service (service differentiation).
  • Positioning is the mental place that differentiation tries to occupy in the customer's mind, relative to competitors. Example: OPPO positions as "Camera Phone"; Nokia positioned as "Connecting People"; Toyota in the Gulf positions as "best AC + durability + fastest spare parts".
The link: differentiation is the cause, positioning is the perceived effect. A company can over-differentiate and under-position (a good product that nobody remembers) or under-differentiate and over-position (a famous slogan with no real product advantage — which doesn't survive the first honest competitor). The goal is to choose a differentiation base, invest in making the difference real, and then communicate it consistently until the market owns the mental position in your favour. 8. Name the 5 requirements for effective market segmentation and give an example of each in practice.
Model answer:
  1. Measurable — you can size the segment. E.g., "there are 4.2 million left-handed Egyptian adults" is measurable; "people with a sense of adventure" is not.
  2. Accessible — you can reach the segment through a channel. E.g., reaching diabetics through pharmacies and endocrinologist clinics.
  3. Substantial — the segment is large enough (or wealthy enough) to be profitable. E.g., "left-handed Egyptians buying premium scissors" might be too small.
  4. Differentiable — the segments respond differently to marketing. E.g., Signal toothpaste's sensitive-teeth variant and whitening-for-smokers variant respond to different ad messaging.
  5. Actionable — you can design an effective marketing programme. E.g., you can actually produce, price, distribute, and promote a product for the segment.
Mnemonic: MASDA. [Slide: Kotler Ch.7 s.17]
